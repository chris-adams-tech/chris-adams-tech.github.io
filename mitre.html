MITRE

1. What is MITRE?

MITRE Corporation is a not-for-profit organization that manages federally funded research and development centers (FFRDCs) and works in the public interest across several sectors, including defense, cybersecurity, healthcare, and more. They primarily collaborate with U.S. government agencies but also work with industry and academic partners to address large-scale problems in these areas.

MITRE is well known in the cybersecurity world for creating the MITRE ATT&CK framework, among other initiatives, but their influence extends beyond that into a variety of critical areas such as:

  • National security: Developing solutions to enhance defense, intelligence, and homeland security.
  • Public health: Addressing medical, health, and safety challenges.
  • Cybersecurity: Creating tools and frameworks to understand, detect, and respond to cyber threats.

2. MITRE's Role in Cybersecurity

MITRE’s involvement in cybersecurity is centered around improving threat detection, incident response, and understanding adversary behavior. Their goal is to help organizations defend against both known and emerging cyber threats by providing open-source frameworks, methodologies, and knowledge bases that are accessible to both public and private sectors.

Their most recognized contributions include:

  • MITRE ATT&CK (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge)
  • CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures)
  • CWE (Common Weakness Enumeration)
  • CAPEC (Common Attack Pattern Enumeration and Classification)

Let’s break down some of these important contributions.

3. MITRE ATT&CK Framework

The MITRE ATT&CK framework is one of MITRE’s most significant contributions to cybersecurity. ATT&CK is a globally accessible knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques based on real-world observations. It is constantly updated as new attack methods and techniques are discovered.

3.1 What is ATT&CK?

  • Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge (ATT&CK) is a curated database that categorizes the behaviors and methods used by threat actors to achieve their objectives during cyber intrusions.
  • ATT&CK focuses on post-compromise techniques—i.e., what attackers do after gaining access to a system, although the framework also includes initial access methods (such as phishing or exploitation).

3.2 The ATT&CK Matrix

The core structure of the framework is the ATT&CK Matrix, which organizes adversary behavior into tactics and techniques:

  • Tactics: Represent the adversary’s goals or intentions (e.g., persistence, privilege escalation, defense evasion, etc.). These tactics describe what attackers want to achieve.
  • Techniques: Describe how adversaries achieve each tactic. Each technique can have sub-techniques, which offer more granularity on specific methods used.
Example:
  • Tactic: Privilege Escalation (the attacker’s goal is to gain higher-level permissions).
  • Technique: Exploitation of Vulnerability (T1068), where attackers exploit a software flaw to escalate privileges.

3.3 ATT&CK's Use in Cybersecurity

ATT&CK provides defenders with a deep understanding of adversary behaviors. It helps in:

  • Threat hunting: Security teams can proactively search for indicators of known attack techniques.
  • Detection and response: By mapping security controls to specific ATT&CK techniques, teams can identify gaps in their detection capabilities.
  • Purple teaming: Red and blue teams use ATT&CK to simulate attacks and test defenses.
  • Security product evaluations: Vendors and enterprises alike use ATT&CK to measure how well security tools detect and mitigate techniques used by adversaries.

3.4 ATT&CK Enterprise, Cloud, and Mobile

ATT&CK is split into different domains:

  • ATT&CK for Enterprise: The most widely used version, focusing on techniques for Windows, Linux, macOS, cloud platforms, and network environments.
  • ATT&CK for Cloud: Covers adversary behaviors specific to cloud environments (e.g., AWS, Azure, Google Cloud).
  • ATT&CK for Mobile: Focuses on techniques used to compromise mobile platforms (iOS and Android).

3.5 ATT&CK for Threat Intelligence

ATT&CK is also used for cyber threat intelligence (CTI), where threat actors are mapped to the tactics and techniques they use. Threat groups like APT29, FIN7, or Emotet have detailed profiles that align their operations with ATT&CK techniques.

4. MITRE CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures)

MITRE also oversees the CVE program, which identifies and catalogs known vulnerabilities in software and hardware systems. Each CVE entry is assigned a unique identifier (e.g., CVE-2023-1234) and provides details about the vulnerability, its potential impact, and remediation options.

  • CVE is critical for vulnerability management, as it helps organizations understand which flaws exist in their systems and prioritize patching efforts.
  • CVE feeds into many security tools (e.g., vulnerability scanners, patch management systems) to help identify exploitable weaknesses in networks.

How CVE works:

  1. Discovery: Security researchers find a vulnerability and report it.
  2. Assignment: The vulnerability is reviewed and assigned a CVE ID.
  3. Disclosure: Information is made public, and vendors typically provide a patch or mitigation plan.

5. MITRE CWE (Common Weakness Enumeration)

The CWE program is another MITRE-led initiative that focuses on software weaknesses rather than specific vulnerabilities. While CVE addresses specific bugs in individual products, CWE identifies classes of software weaknesses that might lead to vulnerabilities (e.g., SQL injection flaws, buffer overflows).

  • CWE provides a structured list of coding errors and architectural weaknesses that developers should avoid.
  • It is used in secure software development lifecycles (SDLCs) to prevent vulnerabilities before they are introduced.

Example CWE:

  • CWE-79: Cross-site Scripting (XSS)
  • CWE-89: SQL Injection

6. MITRE CAPEC (Common Attack Pattern Enumeration and Classification)

CAPEC focuses on classifying common attack patterns. CAPEC provides a dictionary of attack patterns that describe how adversaries exploit weaknesses in systems.

  • CAPEC helps defenders understand attack methods, design countermeasures, and prioritize defenses.
  • It also ties into the ATT&CK framework, where attack patterns are mapped to specific techniques.

Example CAPEC:

  • CAPEC-123: Phishing attack (tricking users into revealing credentials via email).

7. MITRE Engage

MITRE Engage is a newer framework designed to help organizations plan and execute active defense strategies, focusing on engagement with adversaries rather than just passive defense. It complements ATT&CK by providing tactics to engage, confuse, or deceive attackers during the attack.

8. MITRE Shield

MITRE Shield is a knowledge base of active defense techniques that security teams can use to detect, disrupt, or even engage adversaries during their activities. It is like a counterpart to ATT&CK but focused on how defenders can respond and manipulate adversaries during attacks, rather than just detecting or preventing them.

9. MITRE's Influence on the Cybersecurity Ecosystem

MITRE has established itself as a central player in cybersecurity, and its frameworks (especially ATT&CK) are widely adopted by cybersecurity professionals, enterprises, and vendors alike. Some of the ways MITRE's work is utilized include:

  • Security vendors integrating ATT&CK techniques into their products for threat detection, endpoint protection, and SIEM solutions.
  • Government agencies like the U.S. Department of Defense and others using ATT&CK as part of their cyber defense strategies.
  • Industry and academia leveraging MITRE’s frameworks for research, threat modeling, and improving overall security strategies.

10. How to Use MITRE's Resources

MITRE’s frameworks and tools are open-source and can be applied by defenders in various ways:

  • Adopt ATT&CK for threat modeling: Map out how attackers might target your organization and develop defenses aligned with those techniques.
  • Use CVE and CWE for vulnerability management: Prioritize patching efforts and secure development practices.
  • Leverage CAPEC for security awareness training: Educate your teams on common attack vectors and how to prevent them.
  • Test your defenses: Use tools like Atomic Red Team (which integrates ATT&CK) to test how well your organization detects and mitigates attacks based on real-world techniques.

Conclusion

MITRE has become a cornerstone in the cybersecurity community, primarily due to its role in creating the ATT&CK framework, CVE, CWE, and CAPEC programs. These frameworks provide both a tactical and strategic advantage to defenders, helping them understand adversaries' methods, prioritize security efforts, and continuously improve their ability to detect and respond to cyber threats.

The MITRE ATT&CK framework, in particular, is central to modern cybersecurity practices, empowering organizations to map adversarial behaviors, detect threats earlier, and build stronger defenses.
